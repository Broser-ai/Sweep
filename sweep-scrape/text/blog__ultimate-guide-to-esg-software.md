In 2026,
ESG software
has become essential infrastructure for businesses operating in an era of mandatory sustainability reporting, heightened regulatory requirements, and rising stakeholder expectations.
This shift has changed how organizations manage sustainability. ESG reporting is now an ongoing, system-level process that depends on accurate data, clear workflows, and alignment across multiple frameworks.
ESG software sits at the center. It brings together sustainability data from across the business, applies structure to fragmented inputs, and enables reporting that stands up to regulatory and stakeholder scrutiny.
In this guide, you will learn what ESG software is, how it works in practice, the challenges it solves, and what to look for when evaluating solutions.
What is ESG?
ESG
stands for Environmental, Social, and Governance, three core dimensions used to evaluate a company’s sustainability performance, risk exposure, and long-term resilience.
“
Environmental
” covers carbon emissions, carbon footprint, energy consumption, waste management, water usage, biodiversity, and climate risk.
“Social”
includes human resources data, labor practices, diversity and inclusion, human rights, health and safety, and supply chain responsibility.
Governance”
encompasses board structure, executive compensation, ethics, compliance, audit trails, internal controls, and governance data.
Together, these ESG factors form the foundation of corporate sustainability, enabling companies to demonstrate responsible business practices and meet regulatory compliance requirements.
Why should businesses and investors measure ESG data?
ESG data has moved from a voluntary disclosure exercise to a core business requirement, shaped by regulation, investor expectations, and the need for consistent, decision-ready information.
ESG reporting standards and regulatory compliance
Across the European Union, ESG reporting has become mandatory for many companies under frameworks such as the
Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)
. The European Union has developed a robust set of ESG reporting standards in response to the EU Green Deal, reinforcing the need for standardized, auditable disclosures.
In the United States, California climate disclosure laws, including
SB 253
and
SB 261
, are accelerating mandatory reporting expectations, increasing the need for strong data collection, data integration, and audit-ready reporting processes. ESG software automates the creation of reports aligned with frameworks like
GRI
, SASB, and TCFD, helping companies meet regulatory requirements efficiently.
Investor demand and financing access
Institutional investors increasingly prioritize verified ESG performance when allocating capital. ESG credibility can influence financing conditions, cost of capital, and access to capital, particularly as sustainability disclosures become more standardized and comparable.
ESG software builds credibility with investors, customers, and employees by showcasing genuine progress supported by auditable data sources and approval workflows.
Reputation and stakeholder engagement
Stakeholder expectations around sustainability are becoming more demanding and more specific, with greater focus on transparency and data quality. Strong ESG performance, backed by reliable data, supports credible communication and reduces the risk of greenwashing.
ESG reporting solutions enable consistent disclosures across reports and stakeholder audiences.
Risk management
Modern ESG platforms identify and mitigate
ESG-related risks
, including climate impacts, regulatory risk, and supply chain disruption. ESG software also allows companies to model the financial impact of various climate futures and assess business resilience, supporting enterprise risk management and strategic planning.
Operational efficiency and continuous improvement
By automating data collection, streamlining ESG reporting, and integrating diverse datasets across finance systems, ERP platforms, CRM software, HRIS tools, and data warehouses, ESG software reduces manual work and improves consistency. It also provides traceable proof of emissions and human rights data, important for securing market access and maintaining operational continuity.
What are the most important ESG frameworks?
Sustainability reporting is shaped by a growing set of global and regional frameworks. These frameworks define what organizations need to disclose, how data should be structured, and how performance is assessed.
For most companies, the challenge is not choosing a single framework. It is managing overlap across multiple standards while maintaining consistent, audit-ready data.
Below are some of the most widely used frameworks and what they require in practice.
Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)
The
Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive
establishes a standardized EU-wide sustainability reporting regime covering around 50,000 companies. This includes large organizations, listed SMEs, and non-EU companies with significant operations in the region.
CSRD requires detailed disclosures aligned with the European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS), including:
Governance structures and internal controls
Climate transition plans and sustainability strategy
Double materiality assessments (impact and financial risk)
Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions
Targets, KPIs, and progress tracking
Companies must maintain audit-ready data and obtain third-party assurance, increasing the need for structured workflows and traceable data across the organization.
SB 253 and SB 261
SB 253 and SB 261
are California climate disclosure laws that are accelerating mandatory reporting expectations in the United States.
These regulations are pushing organizations to adopt ESG software solutions that ensure compliance with mandatory reporting standards and help avoid potential penalties.
ISSB (IFRS Sustainability Disclosure Standards)
The
International Sustainability Standards Board
(ISSB) provides a global baseline for sustainability disclosures, designed to align ESG reporting more closely with financial reporting.
ISSB standards, including IFRS S1 and S2, focus on:
Financially material sustainability risks and opportunities
Climate-related disclosures aligned with TCFD principles
Integration of sustainability data into financial filings
Consistent, comparable reporting for investors
For multinational organizations, ISSB plays a key role in aligning disclosures across jurisdictions while maintaining investor-grade reporting standards.
Global Reporting Initiative (GRI)
The
Global Reporting Initiative
is one of the most widely adopted frameworks for impact-based sustainability reporting.
GRI focuses on how a company affects the environment and society, rather than just financial risk. It requires disclosures across areas such as:
Emissions, energy use, and environmental impact
Labor practices, human rights, and supply chain responsibility
Governance, ethics, and anti-corruption
GRI is commonly used alongside other frameworks to provide a broader view of sustainability performance and stakeholder impact.
CDP (Carbon Disclosure Project)
CDP
is a global disclosure system used by companies to report environmental data to investors, customers, and regulators.
It is particularly focused on:
Climate change (carbon emissions and reduction strategies)
Water security
Deforestation and supply chain impact
Organizations submit standardized questionnaires, which are scored and used by stakeholders to assess environmental performance and risk exposure.
CDP reporting introduces an additional layer of scrutiny, requiring consistent, high-quality data that aligns with broader ESG disclosures.
EU Taxonomy
The EU Taxonomy defines what qualifies as environmentally sustainable economic activity within the European Union.
It requires companies to disclose:
The proportion of revenue, CapEx, and OpEx aligned with taxonomy criteria
Whether activities meet technical screening thresholds
Alignment with environmental objectives such as climate mitigation and adaptation
This creates a direct link between sustainability performance and financial reporting, increasing the importance of accurate, traceable data.
Why does this create complexity?
Each framework focuses on a different perspective:
CSRD and ESRS define regulatory requirements in the EU
ISSB aligns sustainability with financial reporting
GRI focuses on environmental and social impact
CDP provides standardized environmental disclosure and scoring
EU Taxonomy connects sustainability to financial activity
This is one of the main reasons ESG software has become essential. It allows companies to map a single dataset across multiple frameworks, maintain consistency, and reduce reporting complexity.
How can ESG software help?
Meeting sustainability requirements today involves more than collecting data. Organizations need a structured system that can manage ESG information across teams, frameworks, and reporting cycles.
ESG software provides that foundation by standardizing how data is collected, validated, and reported.
At a practical level, it supports several core functions:
Centralizing sustainability data.
ESG data is brought together from across the business, including finance systems, HR platforms, operational tools, and suppliers. This creates a single, consistent source of truth and reduces fragmentation.
Automating data collection and workflows.
Manual processes are replaced with structured workflows for data requests, approvals, and validation. This improves consistency, reduces errors, and shortens reporting timelines.
Aligning with multiple frameworks.
Data can be mapped to frameworks such as CSRD, GRI, ISSB, and TCFD. This allows the same dataset to be reused across different disclosures without duplicating effort.
Maintaining audit-ready records.
Every data point is traceable, with clear documentation of sources, changes, and approvals. This supports assurance processes and strengthens compliance.
Supporting carbon accounting.
ESG software enables measurement of Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions, including supplier data. This supports emissions reporting, target setting, and decarbonization planning.
Turning data into decision support.
Beyond reporting, ESG data can be analyzed to identify risks, track performance, and inform operational and strategic decisions.
Without this level of structure, ESG reporting becomes difficult to scale and maintain. With it, sustainability data becomes part of how the business operates day-to-day.

Rather than a single category, ESG software typically falls into a few broad groups depending on how organizations approach sustainability data and reporting.

Carbon accounting and climate-focused tools
These tools are centered on emissions measurement, carbon reporting, and decarbonization planning. They are commonly used by organizations prioritizing climate disclosures and reduction strategies.

A cloud-based sustainability management platform that supports ESG reporting by providing collaboration tools and an audit trail. The platform helps mitigate risk and improve productivity, and prides itself on simplifying the reporting process.

How do you measure the ROI of investing in ESG software?
Measuring the ROI of investing in ESG software
involves evaluating both quantitative and qualitative benefits.
Quantitatively, ROI can be assessed by examining cost savings from streamlined data collection and reporting processes, reduced risk of regulatory fines, and increased operational efficiencies.
Additionally, the value of improved stakeholder trust and enhanced investor confidence can be translated into financial terms through metrics such as higher stock valuations or increased investment inflows.
Qualitatively, ROI is measured by the software’s ability to provide actionable insights that drive better decision-making, improve sustainability performance, and align with evolving regulatory requirements. Ultimately, the return on investment is reflected in the software’s contribution to long-term value creation, risk mitigation, and enhanced corporate reputation.
