How to calculate the carbon footprint of a product
A step-by-step guide to calculating your product’s carbon footprint—why it matters, how to do it, and how it helps meet regulations and reduce emissions.
Category
Blog
Last updated
December 10, 2025
Carbon accounting
Whether you’re responding to customer demand, regulatory pressure, or internal sustainability goals, knowing your
product’s carbon footprint
is a powerful step toward more responsible production and decision-making. Understanding the carbon footprint of a product is essential for addressing climate change and reducing climate impacts, as it allows organizations to measure and manage their contributions to global warming. In this blog, we break down what a product carbon footprint is, why it matters, and how you can calculate it in five clear steps.
What is a product carbon footprint?
A
product carbon footprint
is the total emissions of greenhouse gases (GHG) generated across the product’s life cycle. This includes all emissions associated with each stage of the product life cycle, from raw material extraction to disposal. The product carbon footprint consolidates these total emissions, providing a comprehensive view of the product’s environmental impact.
This is typically measured in
carbon dioxide equivalents (CO₂e)
, which includes carbon dioxide emissions as well as other greenhouse gases like methane (CH₄) and nitrous oxide (N₂O), normalized to the impact of CO₂.
The footprint includes emissions from raw material extraction, manufacturing, transportation, use, and end-of-life disposal or recycling. This full product life cycle approach is also referred to as a “cradle-to-grave” assessment. Some companies choose a “cradle-to-gate” boundary, which covers everything from resource extraction to the point the product leaves the factory. Calculating the carbon footprint is based on a product life cycle assessment (LCA), which evaluates the environmental impact of a product throughout its entire supply chain.
Calculating a product’s carbon footprint allows organizations to identify environmental hotspots, optimize processes, and reduce emissions in a meaningful, measurable way. The GHG Protocol Product Standard, as a reporting standard, guides the collection and quality of data for accurate and reliable carbon footprint calculations.
Unlike a product carbon footprint, which focuses on the total emissions from a single product’s life cycle, a
corporate carbon footprint
encompasses all of a company’s operational and value chain emissions across multiple scopes, providing a comprehensive greenhouse gas inventory for the entire organization.
Why is calculating the carbon footprint of your products useful?
There are several compelling reasons to calculate the carbon footprint of your products:
Regulatory compliance
: Emerging regulations like the EU’s Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) and carbon border adjustment mechanisms (CBAM) are increasingly requiring product-level emissions data.
Customer demand
: Consumers and business customers are asking for more transparency around environmental impacts—and they’re more likely to buy from brands that can demonstrate low-carbon credentials.
Supply chain pressure
: Large buyers are starting to require suppliers to disclose product-level emissions as part of their own Scope 3 reporting.
Sustainability strategy
: Knowing which products or components contribute most to your carbon footprint helps prioritize reducing emissions and greenhouse gas emissions, and guide innovation.
Competitive advantage
: Products with lower carbon emissions profiles can stand out in the market, especially as more sectors move toward net zero goals.
Ultimately, a product carbon footprint isn’t just about measurement—it’s a foundation for action.
5 steps to calculating your product carbon footprint
1. Define the product and the scope of the assessment
Start by clearly defining the product you want to assess. This includes identifying:
Functional unit
: What exactly are you measuring? For example, 1 smartphone, 1 kg of packaged pasta, or 1 liter of paint.
System boundaries
: Will your assessment be cradle-to-grave (raw materials to disposal), cradle-to-gate (ending at the factory gate, which marks the endpoint for this boundary and excludes downstream emissions after production), or another scope like gate-to-gate (focused on production only)?
Geographic scope
: Are you including emissions from global operations, or limiting to a specific region? Consider whether to include emissions from other countries, such as those resulting from international trade or imports, when relevant.
Allocation rules
: If your product shares processes or facilities with others, how will you allocate emissions between them?
Establishing these parameters upfront ensures consistency and transparency in your calculations.
2. Map the product lifecycle and value chain
Next, break down the product lifecycle into stages and identify all inputs and activities that contribute to emissions across the supply chains. Common stages include:
Raw material extraction
(e.g., mining, agriculture)
Material processing and component manufacturing
Product assembly
Packaging
Transportation and distribution
Product use
(if it consumes energy or generates emissions)
End-of-life
(disposal, recycling, or incineration)
It is important to include downstream activities—such as delivery to customers, product use, and disposal—and downstream emissions in your assessment, as these can significantly impact the total greenhouse gas emissions associated with a product.
This mapping process is often referred to as a life cycle inventory (LCI) and is a key part of product life cycle accounting. The more detailed and accurate your inventory, the more reliable your footprint will be. All emissions that occur throughout the product’s life cycle should be included in the mapping to ensure comprehensive and transparent reporting.
3. Collect data on materials, processes, and energy use
This is the most data-intensive step. You’ll need to gather:
Primary data
: Direct measurements or activity data from your own operations or suppliers (e.g., electricity use during assembly, materials used, distances shipped, and indirect emissions from your supply chain).
Secondary data
: Emissions factors and lifecycle data from reliable databases like Ecoinvent, ADEME, DEFRA, or commercial lifecycle assessment (LCA) providers. Emissions factors are used to estimate greenhouse gas emissions when primary data is unavailable, providing default or modeled values for various activities and processes.
For each process or material, you’ll want to quantify:
Type and amount of material used
Energy consumption (electricity, fuel, fossil fuels, etc.), while also assessing energy efficiency and identifying opportunities to make processes more energy efficient
Transport mode and distance
Waste generation
Use-phase emissions, if applicable
End-of-life assumptions
If supplier-specific data isn’t available, use industry averages—but document your assumptions.
4. Calculate emissions using emission factors
Once you have the activity data, multiply it by the relevant emission factors to calculate GHG emissions in CO₂e. This process typically involves converting emissions of various greenhouse gases using their global warming potential (GWP) values, so that all emissions are expressed as carbon dioxide equivalent (CO₂e). For example:
Electricity use
: kWh × emission factor (kg CO₂e/kWh)
Material use
: kg of aluminum × emission factor (kg CO₂e/kg)
Transport
: ton-kilometers × emission factor (kg CO₂e/ton-km)
A life cycle assessment (LCA) is often used as the methodology for this calculation, as it measures and analyzes the environmental impacts of products throughout their entire life cycle. LCA helps identify emissions hotspots and guides reduction strategies.
Sum the emissions for each process or lifecycle stage. Be sure to follow consistent units and double-check your math.
You can use spreadsheet tools, software tools such as LCA software (e.g., SimaPro, OpenLCA), or a carbon footprint calculator to manage this step. These software tools and calculators often integrate with emissions databases, streamlining the calculation and providing advanced data analysis and visualization capabilities.
5. Review, verify, and report the results
Finally, review your footprint results for accuracy and completeness. Consider:
Data quality
: Are there gaps? How reliable is your input data?
Assumptions
: Have you documented all key assumptions and boundaries?
Hotspots
: Which stages or components contribute the most to the overall footprint?
If your results will be shared externally—especially in sustainability reports or certifications—it’s a good idea to have them reviewed by an independent third party or verified against standards like ISO 14067 or the GHG Protocol Product Standard.
When reporting your results, include:
Total product footprint (in kg CO₂e per functional unit)
Breakdown by lifecycle stage
Key data sources and assumptions
Improvement opportunities or next steps
This not only enhances transparency but also supports stakeholder trust and informed decision-making.
Final thoughts
Calculating the carbon footprint of your products is a critical step toward environmental accountability and business resilience. It helps you pinpoint inefficiencies, meet growing compliance needs, and demonstrate sustainability leadership in a data-driven way.
While the process can be complex, breaking it down into manageable steps—defining scope, mapping the lifecycle, collecting data, calculating emissions, and reporting results—makes it achievable for companies of all sizes. And with the growing availability of digital tools and emissions databases, it’s easier than ever to build accurate, actionable product carbon footprints.
Whether you’re doing this for one flagship product or across a whole portfolio, remember: carbon data isn’t just for compliance. It’s a lever for innovation, improvement, and real climate impact.
